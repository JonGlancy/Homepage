About Me

A PhD candidate at The University of Sheffield with a background in mathematics and artificial intelligence. I build computational models of animal behaviours in order to explore the relationships between self-organisation and evolution.

Outside of my studies I'm a fencer, I climb fake rocks, and I play board games.

The Model

During my PhD I have developed an agent-based model of thermoregulatory huddling behaviours, such as those seen in small mammals and birds. These behaviours allow a group of animals to work together in order to maintain their core body temperatures, when they might otherwise be unable to do so.

The model is incredibly simple, with each animal being represented by a circle that occupies a 2-dimensional world. These simulated animals are given only two sensors; detecting the temperature on the left and the right side of their body. These sensors are used to determine how the animals should behave according to the following set of rules:

  • If the animal is cold, it will turn towards the side with a higher temperature.
  • If the animal is too hot, it will turn away from the side with a higher temperature.

While this simple behaviour may seem obvious, the group behaviours that emerge are not.
